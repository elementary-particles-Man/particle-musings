From the financial crisis to the food crisis: Examination of the scenario of collapse of three hubs at the same time

Executive Summary: Food Security is the Top-Level Infrastructure

This analysis examines the scenario in which the financial and infrastructure crises occurring at three locations: Chicago (price insurance), California (logistics), and Wall Street (credit), lead to a chain-wise global food crisis, and concludes that this is extremely relevant. The heart of this scenario is that modern food supply networks rely heavily on three pillars: price insurance (hedge), logistics (physical supply), and credit (finance), and if these pillars collapse simultaneously or in a chain, supply to the market will cease regardless of the physical abundance.

Dysfunction at each location is different routes, but amplified one another, paralyzing the food supply network.

  1. Chicago (CME/CBOE) dysfunction disrupts the mechanisms of price discovery and risk transfer. This prevents all participants in the supply chain, from producers to trading companies and processors, from the supply chain to hedge price risk, and as they refrain from trading, the flow of physical goods will immediately stagnate.

  2. California's fiscal and infrastructure crisis cuts off the physical logistics network. A decline in port functions will halt the flow of fresh food and important supplies (fertilizers, agricultural machinery parts), while a weakening of water and electricity infrastructure will threaten production capacity itself.

  3. Wall Street credit contraction will drain funds, which are the bloodstream of the food supply network. The halt of working capital, which is essential for agricultural production and trade finance that supports international trade, leads to dysfunctionality in all processes from production to transportation.

The worst scenario in which these three crises occur simultaneously shows that the financial crisis is not just a market turmoil, but directly leads to an order crisis that shakes the very foundation of food, which is survival. Therefore, the need for a simultaneous backstop to prevent malfunction of each pillar, such as the presented "Food Defense Playbook," is supported as a logical consequence.

I. Three Pillars: Vulnerability Analysis of Food Supply Networks

The presented scenario accurately points out that modern global food supply systems rely on three important nodes (nodes): financial and physical infrastructure. If any of these nodes fail, it can put chain pressure on the other nodes, causing the entire system to collapse.

A. Chicago: The collapse of price insurance (hedge)

Validity assessment: The indication of the immediacy of the scenario is correct. The derivatives market, run by the Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME) Group, is the centre of price discovery and risk management in the global agricultural supply chain. Producers, grain elevators (collectors), food processors and international trading companies use CME's futures and options contracts to hedge future sales and purchase prices to avoid price fluctuations.

Mechanisms for the emergence of crisis: If a systemic financial crisis occurs, as analyzed in the previous report, the impact will immediately spread to CME clearing (central clearing agencies).

  1. Impaired liquidation function: If liquidity across financial markets is exhausted, some major financial institutions that are clearing members of CME Clearing could default. CME Clearing has a robust financial security net called "default waterfall" to cover the losses of defaulted members, but in the event of a large-scale simultaneous default, the risk of its functionality reaching its limits cannot be ruled out.

  2. Surge in margin and trading halt: Once market volatility reaches an extraordinary level, CMEs significantly increase trading margin to manage risk. This will result in many market participants, especially those with little strength, and small and medium-sized businesses, not being able to provide additional margins and be forced to clear their positions or be locked out of new hedging transactions. If extreme price fluctuations continue, trading will be suspended (circuit breakers) will be activated, completely paralyzing market functions.

Immediate impact on agriculture: The supply chain, which has lost its means of hedging, will become dysfunctional.

  • Producers and collectors: As future prices cannot be fixed, there is a polarization of whether to stop sales due to fear of a price drop, or to quickly turn cash into cash and sell. Either way, supply to a stable market will be suspended.

  • Livestock and processed food industry: The inability to hedge the prices of feed grains (corn and soybeans) will force livestock farmers to adjust their production, while processed food manufacturers cannot plan to procure raw materials, resulting in a chain of suspension of production lines and failure to fulfill contracts.

Thus, the collapse of Chicago's financial infrastructure will freeze physical produce trades within hours or days, and will defeat the first dominoes in the supply chain.

B. California: Logistics and Infrastructure Stripping

Validity assessment: In terms of the magnitude of the impact, the scenario with California as its epicenter is extremely serious. California plays an irreplaceable role in food supply not only within the US but also around the world. The state produces more than a third of US vegetables and three-quarters of fruits and nuts, and almonds account for about 80% of the world's supply.

Mechanisms for the emergence of crisis: If the state bond default scenario detailed in the previous report becomes a reality, the impact will directly affect the basic state infrastructure.

  1. Paralysis of port functions: The state's fiscal crisis has hindered the operation of major ports, such as the Port of Los Angeles and Oakland. If infrastructure investment from the state ceases and strikes by dock workers frequently occur, the capacity to unload and unload containers will dramatically decrease. This will slow the supply chain for fresh food imported and exported, as well as fertilizers and agricultural machinery parts, which are essential for agriculture.

  2. Water and electricity supply instability: California agriculture relies heavily on large-scale water infrastructure such as the State Water Project (SWP). The state's financial collapse will stagnate the maintenance and renewal investment of these infrastructures. Additionally, if the reliability of the power grid is reduced, there is a greater risk that refrigerated and frozen facilities and food processing plants will be stopped to maintain the freshness of fresh food.

  3. Agritech investment freeze: California is also a hub for agritech innovation, including precision irrigation and robotic harvesting. These technological developments are supported by funding from venture capital (VCs), but VC investments are the first to shrink during the financial crisis. This will stagnate technological innovation towards improving productivity and stabilizing supply in the future.

Impact on agriculture: California's infrastructure dysfunction is a fatal blow, especially on time-constrained items.

  • Fresh foods: Freshness-rich products, such as fruits, vegetables and dairy products, lose their value after a few days of logistics delay. This will cause items to disappear from supermarket shelves and prices to rise.

  • Impact on import and export: Not only will the export of California nuts and vegetables be slow, but the supply of fertilizer and agricultural machinery parts imported from Asia and elsewhere will also be delayed, which will negatively affect agricultural production in other parts of the United States.

C. Wall Street: Credit evaporation

Validity assessment: The term "human chaos" is appropriate. The credit contraction, which is based on Wall Street, penetrates all aspects of the food supply network and destroys its function from within.

Mechanisms for the emergence of crisis: A systemic financial crisis stimulates the banking system's self-defense instinct and causes a widespread credit crunch.

  1. Agricultural credit suspension: To avoid risk, banks will tighten lending standards and narrow down loans to the agricultural sector, particularly susceptible to economic fluctuations. This will prevent farmers from securing the working capital (sowing funds) needed for planting and harvesting. Even during the 2008 financial crisis, the arrears rate for agricultural loans rose, but remained relatively healthy. However, in a more serious crisis, there is a possibility that refusal to renew loans and suspension of new loans will increase.

  2. Trade Finance Freeze: Many international grain trades rely on trade financing such as letters of credit (L/C). During the financial crisis, the issuance of letters of credit will be suspended due to mutual distrust between banks. This will prevent exporters from taking the risk of collecting prices, and lead to situations all over the world where ships loaded with grain cannot leave ports, or payments cannot be completed and unloaded. In particular, the global dollar liquidity tightness is fatal for emerging countries that import food in dollars.

Global impact: The credit crisis that originates from Wall Street instantly causes global food insecurity.

  • Food import difficulties in emerging countries: As dollar-denominated trade financing stops working, many emerging countries are unable to make food import payments.

  • Inducing export restrictions: Price fluctuations and financial market uncertainty provide incentives for food exporters to prioritize their supply. As was seen during the food price crisis of 2007-08, if countries impose chain-based export restrictions, the amount of food circulating in international markets will decrease further, causing prices to rise even higher.

  • The humanitarian crisis has become more serious: Humanitarian agencies like the World Food Programme (WFP) face sharp rise in procurement costs and instability in supply, making food assistance efforts to the most vulnerable people difficult.

II. Worst scenarios and validity of the locking hand

Completed food crisis

The simultaneous collapse of the three pillars (price insurance, logistics, and credit) presented in the scenario can be said to be the "complete form" of the food crisis. This is not just a food shortage, it is a malfunctioning food supply system. Physically, even if grain exists in the warehouse, it cannot be priced, transport means of movement, and payment cannot be made, so it does not reach consumers. This "quantity is present, but it doesn't reach" situation is the most frightening aspect of the modern food crisis.

Examination of "Food Defense Playbook"

The countermajor presented can be evaluated as a practical and comprehensive countermeasure plan that accurately captures the vulnerabilities of each pillar.

  • Maintaining price insurance (hedge): Emergency liquidity supply to central clearing bodies (CCPs) such as CMEs is extremely important as a last resort to preventing systemic risk. Central banks have the authority and responsibility to provide liquidity to these systemically critical financial market infrastructures (FMIs).

  • Securing logistics and infrastructure: Establishing "food corridors (green lanes)" at ports and prioritizing electricity supply to refrigerated facilities are effective means to ensure the transportation of important supplies in emergencies.

  • Maintaining credit and payments: Automatic extensions of agricultural credit and backstops in trade finance through government guarantees are essential to prevent supply chain disruptions due to credit contraction. The US Farm Credit System (FCS) may be more crisis resistant than commercial banks due to its nature as a government-supported enterprise (GSE), but it requires clear government intervention to support the entire system.

  • Separate damage: Declaring a moratorium on export controls through international frameworks such as the G20 is an important policy coordination derived from lessons from the 2007-08 crisis. The Agricultural Market Information System (AMIS), founded by the G20, is truly a platform to promote this kind of policy coordination.

III. Conclusion: Integrating food security into system risk analysis

As a result of this verification, it was confirmed that the scenario presented has extremely high validity as an inevitable consequence of the previous financial domino scenario. This shows how quickly and directly the financial system crisis threatens food supply, the core of the lives of the people.

"Food is the top security infrastructure" This axiom is logically supported through this analysis. Financial stability, physical infrastructure resilience, and stable food supply should no longer be seen as separate issues, but as one integrated security system that is linked to each other.

Therefore, incorporating the impact on each node of the food supply network (derivative market clearing function, port and logistics capacity, and credit line of agriculture and trade finance) in stress testing of the financial system will be essential for future risk management. The presented monitoring dashboard and trigger-based response action plans (playbooks) are highly regarded as a concrete and practical approach to addressing this new risk paradigm.
